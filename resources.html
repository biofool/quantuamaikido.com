books worth reading

On Aikido

The Art of Peace by Morihei Ueshiba

O Sensei's teachings. Essential. Read it slowly. Sit with it.

Aikido and the Dynamic Sphere by Adele Westbrook and Oscar Ratti

Best technical book on Aikido. Detailed illustrations. Clear explanations.

The Sword of No-Sword by John Stevens

Biography of Morihei Ueshiba. The man behind the art.

On Quantum Mechanics (for humans)

The Quantum World by J.C. Polkinghorne

Accessible without dumbing down. Good bridge between physics and philosophy.

The Dancing Wu Li Masters by Gary Zukav

Older but still relevant. Makes quantum mechanics feel less alien.

Quantum Theory Cannot Hurt You by Marcus Chown

Playful. Clear. Doesn't require physics background.

On Consciousness

The Master and His Emissary by Iain McGilchrist

Left brain/right brain. How we've gotten unbalanced. Dense but worth it.

Waking Up by Sam Harris

Secular approach to consciousness exploration. Cuts through new age fluff.

The Body Keeps the Score by Bessel van der Kolk

Trauma, soma, healing. Neuroscience of how we hold experience.

On Conflict & Communication

Difficult Conversations by Douglas Stone, Bruce Patton, Sheila Heen

Harvard Negotiation Project work. Practical. Real-world applicable.

Getting to Yes by Roger Fisher and William Ury

Classic negotiation text. Principle-based approach.

Nonviolent Communication by Marshall Rosenberg

Sometimes misunderstood as soft. Actually fierce when done right.

On Practice & Mastery

Mastery by George Leonard

My teacher's teacher. About the path, not the destination.

Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind by Shunryu Suzuki

Essential. Simple. Profound. Return to it repeatedly.

The War of Art by Steven Pressfield

Resistance. Creativity. Showing up. For anyone making anything.

Poetry

Complete Poems by e.e. cummings

Form breaking form. The way he sees.

Rumi - Any translation by Coleman Barks

The drunk monk. Mystic love. Energy in words.

Devotions by Mary Oliver

Paying attention. Being alive. Simple depth.

finding teachers

Aikido Dojos

Not all Aikido is the same. Different styles. Different emphasis.

Look for:

  • Lineage to O Sensei (most legitimate dojos have this)
  • Emphasis on principle over technique
  • Teacher who practices what they teach
  • Students who've been there years
  • Clean mat, respectful atmosphere

Red flags:

  • Cult-like devotion to sensei
  • No connection to larger Aikido community
  • Too many fees beyond regular tuition
  • Disrespect toward other martial arts
  • Pressure to advance in rank quickly

Aikido Resources

  • Aikido Journal - History, interviews, technique
  • Aikido Sangenkai - Deep dives into principles
  • Aikiweb - Forums, community, discussion

meditation & practice resources

Apps

Insight Timer

Free. Huge library. Good guided meditations without corporate mindfulness vibe.

Waking Up

Sam Harris's app. Secular. Rigorous. Theory and practice integrated.

Oak

Simple. Free. Breathing exercises. Nothing fancy.

Breathing

Breath by James Nestor

How we've forgotten to breathe properly. Surprisingly fascinating.

Box breathing technique: 4 counts in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold. Repeat.

Physiological sigh: Double inhale through nose, long exhale through mouth.

Both free. Both effective.

what not to waste time on

  • Generic mindfulness apps promising enlightenment in 10 days.
  • Quantum mysticism that confuses quantum mechanics with wish fulfillment.
  • Self-help promising easy answers.
  • Any teacher who claims perfection or wants worship.
  • Books that repeat the same idea for 300 pages (could've been an article).
  • Certification programs that certify after a weekend.

If it sounds too easy, it is.
If it promises quick transformation, it won't deliver.
If it asks you to stop thinking critically, run.

a note on resources

Resources point. They don't do the work for you.

You can read every book on this page and remain unchanged.
You can train in a dojo for years and miss the point.
You can meditate daily and stay stuck in the same patterns.

Information isn't transformation.
Understanding isn't embodiment.
Knowing isn't doing.

Use resources to support practice.
Use practice to test understanding.
Use understanding to deepen practice.

Repeat forever.
